Classical coda theory assumption:
1.  Stationary medium (g0 and Qi-1)
2.  Statistical averaging
Thus, coda decay slope is stable
--\> Qc is treated as regional constant

Multiple studies reported that the slope of coda decay curve changes with time
Cases:
- Precede large earthquake (Jin and Aki 1986, Gusev and Lemzikov 1985, sato 1987)
- Accompany volcanic eruption (fehler 1988)
- Recover afterward

Physical interpretation
Qc Increase
- Increased intrinsic adsorption (fluid, temperature, partial melt)
- Increased small scale cracking
--\> often reported before earthquakes and volcanic eruptions
Qc decrease
- Reduced adsorption
- Cracks healing
- Stress relaxation
--\> often observed after mainshocks and eruptions

Prone to bias (criticisms)
if lapse time window is not constant (mix early and late coda) --\> artificially create temporal Qc change
Contamination possibilities:
- Changing focal mechanism
- Changing source depth
--\> recent measurement has more attention to lapse-time window and focal depths

Temporal changes in Qc are real in some cases, but subtle and easily misinterpreted.
Signs of reported Qc changes are not systematic
--\> some of them increase, other no change (inconsistent)
They likely reflect
- Gradual damage accumulation
- Fluid migration
- Crack density evolution
- Ductile-brittle coupling
~not an universal earthquake precursor

**Doublet analysis**
Assumption --\> coda shape is stable and independent of source mechanism and location (in a statistical sense).

Got et al 1990
--\> small changes in earthquake kinematics can create variations in coda excitation that mimic apparent Qc changes.
Comparing earthquakes with different focal mechanisms, location, raypath --\> falsely interpret source effects as temporal changes in the medium
Using doublets --\> identical path, radiation pattern, site effects --\> coda decay attributed to changes in the medium
Ratio of doublets will cancels:
- Site amplification --\> same station
- Path effects --\> same location
- Power law decay --\> same lapse time
- Scattering geometry --\> same medium geometry
Physical interpretation
If temporal change occurs, such as:
- Crack density inrease
- Fluid migration
- Volcanic inflation
- Damage accumulation before rupture
Then:
- Later event coda decays faster
- Qc increases
Help solve previous controversy of prone to bias
--\> stated that many early claims were over-interpreted, changes are often below 5-10%
